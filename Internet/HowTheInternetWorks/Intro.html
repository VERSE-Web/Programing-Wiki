What is the Internet?

The Internet is a global system of interconnected computer networks that lets devices communicate and share information worldwide. It runs on the TCP/IP suite, which defines how data is addressed, sent, and received.

History

The roots of the Internet trace to the late 1960s with ARPANET, a U.S. research project aimed at creating resilient, decentralized communications. Through the 1970s and 1980s, academic, government, and commercial networks adopted TCP/IP and interconnected. By the early 1990s, public access expanded rapidly, transforming the Internet into a mainstream global network.

How It Works

The Internet is a network of networks. Each connected device has a unique IP address. Information is split into small packets that travel across routers, switches, and long-haul links (fiber-optic cables, satellites, undersea cables, and wireless).

  • Servers – host data and services.
  • Clients – devices (laptops, phones) that request information.
  • Infrastructure – the physical links and hardware that carry data.

Internet vs. World Wide Web

The Internet is the underlying global network infrastructure. The World Wide Web (WWW) is a collection of interlinked documents and apps that run on top of the Internet, accessed through web browsers using HTTP or HTTPS.

Beyond the Web, the Internet also powers:

  • Email (SMTP, IMAP, POP3)
  • File transfer (FTP, cloud storage)
  • Messaging and VoIP
  • Online gaming and real-time media
  • APIs and machine-to-machine communications (IoT)

Modern Impact

Billions of people use the Internet daily for communication, commerce, education, entertainment, and research. Its reach keeps expanding with fiber-optic broadband, 5G, and satellite networks.
